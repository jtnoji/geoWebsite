The core change: the answer replaced the list. Traditional search gave your customer ten links and let them choose; AI search hands them a written answer that names two or three businesses. Fewer winners per query, no page two, and the "result" varies from one run to the next — which changes both how visibility works and how it has to be measured.

## Is SEO dead, then?

No — much of what made sites rank still feeds the engines: crawlable pages, real content, consistent business information, presence on trusted sources. What died is the measurement model. A "rank" is a stable position on a page; an AI answer is a sample from a distribution. Google now shows an AI-generated answer on roughly 2 of 3 informational searches (Whitespark, 2025), so the paradigm shift is already the default experience.

## What matters more in AI search than it did in SEO?

Three things stand out. Being on the shortlist of sources engines cite in your category matters more than accumulating links broadly. Directly answering real customer questions matters more than keyword coverage — engines quote pages that answer plainly. And accuracy is now a visibility issue: an engine can name you and get your prices or services wrong, something a blue link could never do.

## How do I measure visibility when every answer is different?

By sampling. Run the same customer questions many times per engine and report rates — "named in 7 of 10 runs" — instead of a single-fetch "AI rank," which is a coin flip dressed up as data. Rates can be tracked over time, compared against competitors, and honestly show their own uncertainty.

## What should a business do differently this year?

Verify AI crawlers can read your site, find out which sources your engines cite and make sure they know you, publish pages that answer your customers' actual questions in the first sentence — and measure before and after every change. The businesses that win AI search treat it as something to measure, not something to guess about.
